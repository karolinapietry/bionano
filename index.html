Challenge

Each drop of seawater contains thousands of cells that define the biodiversity of life in our seas and oceans. Scientists for many years have been conducting time-consuming and very costly research into the so-called eDNA forms of marine life. Additionally, single scientists may recognize differences between organisms, but they may not recognize different levels of biodiversity and why these levels matter. The different levels of biodiversity include ecosystem diversity, species diversity, and genetic diversity.

Accuracy of research results is crucial to generate appropriate conclusions and recommendations constituting the basis of protection policies in order to prevent dangerous worldwide species extinction phenomena due to negative environmental factors. For several years, research projects have been carried out all over the planet, aimed at developing autonomous methods of collecting seawater samples for eDNA analysis, which will radically increase the size of data. Unfortunately, it is impossible due to the use of current high-cost manual methods.

Biodiversity importance

All species are an integral part of their ecosystem by performing specific functions that are often essential to their ecosystems and often to human survival as well. Removing species from ecosystems removes those important functions. Therefore, the greater the diversity of an ecosystem the better it can maintain balance and productivity and withstand environmental stressors.

In the ocean ecosystem species diversity is limited, for example, the sea otter population in southern California was greatly impacted by the fur trade, which resulted in a sea urchin population explosion. The sea urchins then devastated many kelp forests in southern California. Likewise, genetic diversity ensures that when given environmental pressures occur, a proportion of species will continue to reproduce and survive the change in the environment.
